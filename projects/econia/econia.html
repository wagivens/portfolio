Overview

Problem: Young adults are often confused about which products to recycle and how to recycle them due to the wide range of recyclable materials and recycling parameters. As such, recyclable waste ends up in landfills instead of recycling centers. How might we streamline the recycling process for young adults while also educating them on the environmental impact of the products they use?

I was a designer for this project

August 2020 - November 2020

Myself and 4 peers

This project was not built/developed

The Original Solution

My team and I utilized the Design Thinking Framework throughout the course of one semester to create a mobile application that would simultaneously educate users and incentivize them to recycle.

Research

Before jumping into generative research, we thought about what questions we needed to answer (research objectives) and the best ways to answer them (research methods). We needed information about our market, as well as a mix of attitudinal and behavioral data about our target user group, so we focused on methods that would help us with that.

Our main research objectives were to:

  • Understand people's current recycling habits.
  • Identify how recycling habits can be changed or improved.
  • Examine people's knowledge of recycling materials.

Some of the questions we would need to answer to reach those objectives:

  • How often do people recycle?
  • How do people decide which items to recycle?
  • What does the end-to-end process of recycling look like for most people?
  • Do people care about sustainability in other areas (ex: reusable bags, low water usage)?
  • Are people enrolled in any rewards programs (ex: Starbucks Rewards)? Why or why not?

We understood our data should be both qualitative and quantitative, so we decided on doing a competitive analysis, user interviews, and a survey. The competitive analysis would provide useful data about the market, while the interviews and survey would give us insight into our target users' behavior and attitudes.

By comparing the features, strengths, and weaknesses of different products, we discovered that many recycling apps/sites are great at educating users and providing local resources, as well as having enjoyable user experiences. However, they are solely educational and lack gamification and incentives to motivate users to apply their recycling knowledge.

Our interviews gave us deeper attitudinal and behavioral insights about young adults. Young adults are: 1) often influenced by peer pressure to recycle more, 2) enthusiastic about sustainable/reusable alternatives to products they commonly recycle, and 3) interested in lifestyle apps they can easily integrate into their daily routines.

We spent time thoroughly analyzing our survey results. 71% of respondents reported feeling positive towards recycling, but only 28% felt like their knowledge of recycling was extensive. 92% reported receiving no compensation for recycling, and a majority (69%) said they would recycle more if they received something in return. Over half (57%) said they never pay attention to codes on products when recycling.

Using the competitive analysis, as well as data from the survey and interviews, we concluded:

  • Our product needed to further educate users on recyclable products and the recycling process.
  • We would also need to provide healthy peer pressure and relevant incentives for participating.

Ideating Potential Solutions

The first step we took in our ideation process was completing empathy maps for our two main types of users; this would let us put ourselves in the shoes of our users in a deeper way. Our primary user was a young adult who greatly favors recycling but believes the process is not streamlined enough. Our secondary user felt indifferent towards recycling but does it out of convenience and peer pressure.

We also proposed some "How Might We?" questions and dot-voting which themes were most common among us. Our most popular ones revolved around educating users, providing incentives, and integrating easily into users' lives, so we decided to explore those ideas further.

Prototyping Final Solutions

Before prototyping, we constructed a sitemap and wireframes of our application.

Then, we put everything together to create our high-fidelity prototypes. Here is a brief breakdown of the key features:

  • The Home screen presents users with a list of other people's recycling activity to promote healthy peer pressure.
  • People would start the recycling process by scanning a recyclable item, after which they could save it to their list to recycle later (if they couldn't in the moment) or locate nearby bins to deposit the item.
  • To successfully complete a “recycle”, people are given a map of nearby recycling bins. Upon arriving, they scan a QR code (letting us know the item deposit was legitimate) and are then awarded points.
  • Econia's Profile page shows users their points and suggests sustainable alternatives to products they recycle often (ex: water bottles). The Rewards page offers prizes relevant to our target audience, which users can buy with their recycling points.

Testing Our Solutions

Our evaluative research centered around simply testing our prototype and determining if we addressed the pain points our target user group identified in our generative research. We decided the best method would be 5 remote moderated usability tests conducted over Zoom and using our prototype on Figma.

To properly conduct our usability tests, we constructed a script that each team member would use when interviewing. The script would include scenarios involving important user flows, and we would be able to measure the effectiveness/intuitiveness of our solutions by how well our testers did with the scenarios.

We debriefed with testers after each scenario, asking them questions about their experience (ex: "Did you expect for that to happen when you clicked there?", "What did you think that icon meant before clicking on it?").

Our remote moderated usability tests taught us:

  • Clear iconography is important. 3 of our participants thought our icons were confusing.
  • User onboarding can be valuable. Some interviewees expressed they would have been more comfortable navigating the application if they completed onboarding first.
  • The Rewards section of our app was well-received, as were the educational tips and recyclable product recommendations.

My Redesign

Since Econia was never intended to go into production, I decided to redesign it independently to see how I could improve it. I made many purely aesthetic modifications, but I based most other refinements on the experience as a whole.

One change was placing the user's points at the top, making it always visible instead of only accessible on the Profile and Rewards pages. I also moved the nav bar to the bottom of the screen both for better ergonomics and to be more consistent with other applications (we, as users, are familiar with nav bars being at the bottom).

I made the home screen more of a leaderboard instead of a constant stream of activity from nearby users; the folks using the app likely would only want to see a total number of points, not every recycle another user is making. Additionally, I refined the visual design of the List page while retaining the ability for users to learn more about what they are recycling.

I overhauled the Rewards page to look more aesthetically pleasing, as well as make it easier for a user to save and redeem an offer.
