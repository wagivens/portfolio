Econia

Living sustainably is becoming increasingly important as the effects of climate change become more apparent. Young adults enjoy trying to live green, particularly by recycling, but the process can be confusing and the benefits are not always clear. This prompted a team of myself and 4 others, as part of a university course, to remedy those issues with a new digital product: Econia.

Overview

Problem: Young adults are often confused about which products to recycle and how to recycle them due to the wide range of recyclable materials and recycling parameters. As such, recyclable waste ends up in landfills instead of recycling centers.

Timeline and Disciplines

August 2020 - November 2020

Design Thinking/Process, User Research, Wireframing, Prototyping, Usability Testing, Interaction Design, Visual Design

The Original Solution

My team and I utilized the Design Thinking Framework throughout the course of one semester to create a mobile application that would simultaneously educate users and incentivize them to recycle.

Defining the Product

We began with a competitive analysis on recycling apps and sites. By comparing the features, strengths, and weaknesses of different products, we discovered that many recycling apps/sites are great at educating users and providing local resources, as well as having enjoyable user experiences. However, they are purely educational and lack incentives to motivate users to apply their recycling knowledge.

Empathizing With Target Users

We then moved on to gaining an understanding of our target users. We believed a user survey coupled with five interviews would give us the best insights.

Then, we analyzed the 70 responses we received on our survey:

  • 71% of respondents reported feeling positive towards recycling, but only 28% felt like their knowledge of recycling was extensive.
  • 92% reported receiving no compensation for recycling, and a majority (69%) said they would recycle more if they received something in return.
  • Over half (57%) said they never pay attention to codes on products when recycling.

Our interviews taught us that young adults are:

  • Often influenced by peer pressure to recycle more.
  • Enthusiastic about sustainable/reusable alternatives to products they commonly recycle.
  • Interested in lifestyle apps they can easily integrate into their daily routines.

Using our competitive analysis and research, we decided it was imperative for our product to further educate users on recyclable products and the recycling process. We would also need to provide healthy peer pressure and relevant incentives for participating.

From there, we completed empathy maps for our two main types of users. Our primary user was a young adult who greatly favors recycling but believes the process is not streamlined enough. Our secondary user felt indifferent towards recycling but does it out of convenience and peer pressure.

Prototyping Final Solutions

Before prototyping, we constructed a sitemap and wireframes of our application.

Then, we put everything together to create our high-fidelity prototypes. Here is a brief breakdown of the key features:

  • The Home screen presents users with a list of other people's recycling activity to promote healthy peer pressure.
  • People would start the recycling process by scanning a recyclable item, after which they could save it to their list to recycle later (if they couldn't in the moment) or locate nearby bins to deposit the item.
  • To successfully complete a “recycle”, people are given a map of nearby recycling bins. Upon arriving, they scan a QR code (letting us know the item deposit was legitimate) and are then awarded points.
  • Econia's Profile page shows users their points and suggests sustainable alternatives to products they recycle often (ex: water bottles). The Rewards page offers prizes relevant to our target audience, which users can buy with their recycling points.

Testing Our Solutions

We conducted 5 usability tests through observation and interviewing, which ultimately taught us:

  • Clear iconography is important. 3 of our participants thought our icons were confusing.
  • User onboarding can be valuable. Some interviewees expressed they would have been more comfortable navigating the application if they completed onboarding first.
  • The Rewards section of our app was well-received, as were the educational tips and recyclable product recommendations.

My Redesign

Since Econia was never intended to go into production, I decided to redesign it independently to see how I could improve it. I made many purely aesthetic modifications, but I based most other refinements on the experience as a whole.

One change was placing the user's points at the top, making it always visible instead of only accessible on the Profile and Rewards pages. I also moved the nav bar to the bottom of the screen both for better ergonomics and to be more consistent with other applications (we, as users, are familiar with nav bars being at the bottom).

I made the home screen more of a leaderboardinstead of a constant stream of activity from nearby users; the folks using the app likely would only want to see a total number of points, not every recycle another user is making. Additionally, I refined the visual design of the List page while retaining the ability for users to learn more about what they are recycling.

I overhauled the Rewards page to look more aesthetically pleasing, as well as make it easier for a user to save and redeem an offer.
