Trip Planning on Notion

I have never been able to travel outside of my home state, so I have a lot of places I want to visit. One day, I decided to start collating my trip ideas in Notion instead of trying to keep them all in my head; I listed out every location along with potential hotels to stay at, sites to see, and restaurants to dine at.

It became a bit cumbersome to use. After a while, I thought, “This is probably how lots of people plan their trips, but there might be a cleaner, more streamlined way.” Thus, the task for me was to create a simple, lightweight way for people to organize their plans for trips around the world, and Chronacle was born.

Exploratory Research

In this stage of early, generative research, I mostly focused on attitudinal methods and analyzing how people feel about trip planning and their resulting trips; I thought this would give me a great sense of what people care about when planning trips and their reception to existing tools. Also, behavioral methods would have been impractical because I wasn't able to observe people while they were planning or taking their trips.

What I want to know (research objectives):

  1. Determine how people are already planning trips, and how they feel about it.
  2. Understand the typical structure of people's trips (location, length, budget, activities, etc).
  3. Discover people's typical motivations behind taking trips and how they feel about recent trips they've taken.

Some questions I need to answer to reach objectives:

  1. How far in advance are people planning?
  2. How important is cost when trip planning?
  3. What is the average length of people's trips?
  4. Where are people making trips to (local, national, international)?
  5. What kinds of activities, restaurants, sights, etc. are people saving before traveling? Where do they save them?
  6. How many trips are people planning each year?

Secondary Research

As the sole designer, I figured I could first leverage some secondary research from a reputable consumer research organization like Mintel. This gave me the attitudinal data I was looking for, but also insights into people's behavior regarding their trips. Here's what I learned:

People are taking fewer trips post-pandemic, and trips are shorter. Most people planned to take between 1 and 3 trips throughout 2021, and nearly half of the people surveyed (46%) planned for trips to be between 1 and 3 days long. The next largest group (38%) planned for their trips to be between 4 and 7 days. Lower-income households (less than $75k income) had shorter trips, only planning for trips between 1 and 3 days.

My Takeaway: The small number of trips and the limited time spent away might mean people will want to maximize their trip schedules. Also, since lower-income homes are staying for shorter periods, they will need to be able to easily see the cost of food and activities, and maybe even the average time activities require

Folks are staying in their countries and taking vacations to familiar places; 68% of people surveyed said recent trips were to visit family, and 58% said trips were to elsewhere in the U.S. Only 17% went somewhere new, and even fewer (13%) to a place outside of the U.S.

My Takeaway: People are reluctant to visit places they know nothing about, so I could give suggestions for things like landmarks, restaurants, and entertainment instead of leaving them to find and list things out. This gives them some pre-trip comfort and allows them to freely change plans without consequence

People are increasingly using their mobile devices to plan trips, and are increasingly booking accommodations through brand apps instead of online travel agencies like TripAdvisor. They are more likely to use online travel agencies for activities instead of for accommodations.

My Takeaway: I don't need to focus on providing ways to book accommodations like hotels, flights, rental cars, etc. since people will use their preferred brands' apps to do so. Chronacle should focus on activities/experiences you can't reserve in advance and things you wouldn't reserve on an app, such as restaurant reservations

People want flexibility in their trips. 53% of people surveyed wanted travel booking apps to show lower-cost alternatives to their dates and times. The same amount of people said they wanted a flexible booking calendar. Safety ratings and personalizations were desired features as well, with 36% and 31% of people saying they wanted them, respectively.

My Takeaway: People care about flexibility and cost, so Chronacle should provide the price of activities, entertainment, food, etc. and present all options. It should also give people a sense of freedom by allowing them to freely add, remove, and rearrange the things they add to their trip. Since personalization is important, Chronacle should let people both search for things they want to see/do as well as add them to a personal “trip” they can keep referencing and changing.

People are planning closer to trip dates--sometimes not until days or weeks beforehand--to take advantage of deals and discounts. However, things like flights and hotels are often first booked about a month in advance.

My Takeaway: This is more evidence that flexibility is very important for people when planning trips. Chronacle should aim to allow as much freedom and flexibility for users as possible

Source: Mintel. (2022). Travel Booking: Direct Vs OTAs US, 2022 -- March 2022. Retrieved April 28, 2022 from Mintel Reports database.

Competitive Analysis

The Mintel study gave insight into which platforms people are using to outline their trips, so I used that information to conduct a competitive analysis; I was able to pinpoint common features and user flows as well as identify opportunities for differentiation. This competitive analysis was largely for generative reasons and to explore crucial features; I only focused on particular heuristics like Flexibility and Efficiency since they directly related to my secondary research findings.

The four key players in this space are Booking Holdings (creator of booking.com), Expedia Group, Airbnb, and TripAdvisor. Since Booking Holdings and Airbnb's products are more centered on accommodations, I decided to review Expedia and TripAdvisor's products along with other direct competitors TripScout and Wanderlog.

Many of them were efficient, particularly in helping me find popular activities and businesses; they presented suggested activities immediately and allowed me to tap to save individual ones for my trip.

Expedia could improve flexibility. Unlike other competitors, I wasn't able to search for specific businesses or sights in cities; instead, I could only see events and activities within a certain time period.

After examining the applications from competitors, there were four essential features and flows that I encountered and wanted to focus on for Chronacle:

  1. A primary “Trip” page that presents the user with an overview of their trip, as well as an itinerary section for users to organize their desired activities.
  2. A section including suggested or popular activities in location, allowing users to quickly add popular sites to their trip plans.
  3. An “Explore” page for users to search for various things to do in a particular location.
  4. A page essentially giving an overview of a specific business or activity. These pages typically include information such as a business's/activity's address, phone number, and operating hours, as well as photos.

Refinements

Of course, your first solution(s) are hardly ever your best, and there is always room to iterate and improve. Even after completing the high-fidelity designs for Chronacle, I noticed there were some elements of the product that could be refined.

Here is what I changed:

  1. For the main Trip page, I refined the itinerary date list to be cleaner, changed the activity rating to be more consistent with the Explore page, and moved all activity actions into their own menu.
  2. Within the Explore page, I made use of breadcrumbs instead of buttons. This lets the user know how far into the Explore section they are and provides easy backtracking.
  3. When a user is searching for activities, results are segmented using pagination. I decided on this instead of a “load more” button so users can know which page they found certain activities on.

Next Steps

Now that the designs for Chronacle are mostly finished, I am working on:

  1. Putting together a design system in Storybook. Using Storybook to create a design system will allow me to have a single source of truth for various aspects of the product (visual, layout, components, etc).
  2. Development. I'm excited to make use of Design Tokens and Web Components when developing Chronacle. Design Tokens will allow me to easily incorporate design decisions into my development workflow; I'll only have to change design decisions in one place and it will cascade through the entire design system/product. Web Components are an exciting addition to the JavaScript language, and tooling for them is growing as they become more popular. I will specifically be using the Lit framework to develop Chronacle's components.
