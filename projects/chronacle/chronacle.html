Building Chronacle

Up until July 2023, at the age of 24, I had never owned a passport. Having never been on a plane (or even inside an airport), I spent many years listing out places I wanted to visit, as well as everything I wanted to do when I got there. Over time, this list became clunky and cumbersome to use, and I longed for something more engaging or personalized; I also figured this is the way many people plan their trips, so a solution for this would be interesting to put together. In 2021, I set out to craft a fun, easy way to document one's journeys around the world.

Generative research showed me folks are taking fewer, shorter trips post-pandemic and only traveling to familiar places. Travelers are reluctant to visit new places but need helpful starting points when they do go somewhere new; they want to see as many options as possible for things to do. People plan trips mere days or weeks before leaving, and use their preferred brands' sites (ex: Marriott Hotels) to book accommodations like hotels/flights/rental cars instead of using travel agency sites (ex: TripAdvisor).

I decided the focus for Chronacle should be on activities and experiences you might not reserve in advance, or for things you wouldn't know about unless you lived in that place; Chronacle would provide ample suggestions for amenities, venues, restaurants, landmarks, and more. People want flexibility, so Chronacle should be about helping folks easily map out their trips, as well as provide the freedom to add/remove/rearrange things on their itineraries.

Searching for a restaurant to add.

The goal with Chronacle's flow was to make it super easy to jump right into planning a trip. Once someone puts in a location, Chronacle generates a trip page with a hero photo, activity suggestions, and an itinerary.

For the desktop view, I wanted Chronacle to feel like a canvas. Much of the design for the desktop view is about using the canvas itself to communicate information, as well as find ways to provide fun microinteractions that make use of space/areas web applications typically don't use.

Experimenting with using the canvas as a loading indicator.

In regards to development, I wanted Chronacle to be a progressive web application (PWA) so that it would be available to as many people as possible and not tethered to a particular operating system. PWAs are also installable, providing a native look and feel while allowing me to leverage newer web and design technologies like web components and design tokens.

Chronacle's design system is being made with Storybook, allowing me to keep future designs consistent, as well as make development simple and seamless.
