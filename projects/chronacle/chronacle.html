Overview

Trip Planning on Notion

I have never been able to travel outside of my home state, so I have a lot of places I want to visit. One day, I decided to start collating my trip ideas in Notion instead of trying to keep them all in my head; I listed out every location along with potential hotels to stay at, sites to see, and restaurants to dine at.

It became a bit cumbersome to use. After a while, I thought, “This is probably how lots of people plan their trips, but there might be a cleaner, more streamlined way.” Thus, the task for me was tocreate a simple, lightweight way for people to organize their plans for trips around the world, and Chronacle was born.

Working Through Ambiguity

In practice, you may not always have access to robust research and user insights before designing something new or iterating on a design. I wanted to challenge myself by designing something with ambiguous constraints; what does the design process look like with limited information about your target users and their motivations?

User Interview

At this point in the design process, you're likely conducting interviews with folks you think are part of your target user group. I decided I would do a short interview with only one person to discover more about their trip-planning process. This person:

  • Looks through trip planning applications (ex: TripAdvisor) beforehand for activities they can do while there.
  • Considers the cost and safety of each activity, as well as the reviews. Reputation is important for places they have never been to.
  • Tends to save plans (ex: sites they want to see) to their phone or a note-taking application online (like Notion).

These insights were great, but obviously not enough. I decided to also complete a competitive analysis, reviewing applications from competitors (Trip Advisor, Wanderlog, and TripScout) and analyzing their features/user flows.

Competitive Analysis

After examining the applications from the three competitors, I noted what I believed to be the most important user flows and features shared among them:

  • A primary “Trip” page that presents the user with an overview of their trip, as well as an itinerary section for users to organize their desired activities.
  • A section including suggested or popular activities in location, allowing users to quickly add popular sites to their trip plans.
  • An “Explore” page for users to search for various things to do in a particular location.
  • A page essentially giving an overview of a specific business or activity. These pages typically include information such as a business's/activity's address, phone number, and operating hours, as well as photos.

These four flows were what I decided to primarily focus on for Chronacle.

Refinements

Of course, your first solution(s) are hardly ever your best, and there is always room to iterate and improve. Even after completing the high-fidelity designs for Chronacle, I noticed there were some elements of the product that could be refined.

Here is what I changed:

  • For the main Trip page, I refined the itinerary date list to be cleaner, changed the activity rating to be more consistent with the Explore page, and moved all activity actions into their own menu.
  • Within the Explore page, I made use of breadcrumbs instead of buttons. This lets the user know how far into the Explore section they are and provides easy backtracking.
  • When a user is searching for activities, results are segmented using pagination. I decided on this instead of a “load more” button so users can know which page they found certain activities on.

Next Steps

Now that the designs for Chronacle are finished, my next tasks will be:

  • Usability testing. This is of utmost importance since I can learn the most about my current designs by simply testing them, allowing me to iterate as needed.
  • Putting together a design system in Storybook. Using Storybook to create a design system will allow me to have a single source of truth for various aspects of the product (visual, layout, components, etc).
  • Development. I'm excited to make use of Design Tokens and Web Components when developing Chronacle. Design Tokens will allow me to easily incorporate design decisions into my development workflow; I'll only have to change design decisions in one place and it will cascade through the entire design system/product. Web Components are an exciting addition to the JavaScript language, and tooling for them is growing as they become more popular. I will specifically be using the Lit framework to develop Chronacle's components.
